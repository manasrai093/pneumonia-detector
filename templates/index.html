Understanding Pneumonia

A serious lung infection affecting millions worldwide — early detection saves lives.

What Is Pneumonia?

Pneumonia is an infection that inflames the air sacs (alveoli) in one or both lungs. The air sacs may fill with fluid or pus, causing symptoms such as a cough, fever, chills, and difficulty breathing.

It can range from mild to life-threatening and is most serious for infants, young children, people older than 65, and individuals with weakened immune systems or chronic conditions.

Clinical Signs

Recognizing the Symptoms

Early recognition of symptoms is critical for timely treatment and recovery.

High Fever & Chills

A sudden high fever (38°C / 100.4°F or above), often accompanied by shaking chills and profuse sweating. Fever is the body's immune response to infection.

Persistent Cough

A cough that produces thick, greenish, yellowish, or blood-tinged phlegm (mucus). Coughing is the body's mechanism to clear the lungs of fluid.

Shortness of Breath

Difficulty breathing, rapid shallow breaths, or feeling like you can't get enough air — even when resting or doing minimal activity.

Chest Pain

Sharp or stabbing chest pain that worsens when breathing deeply or coughing, caused by inflammation of the pleura (lining around the lungs).

Fatigue & Weakness

Extreme tiredness, low energy, and muscle aches that make it difficult to perform daily activities. The body directs energy to fighting the infection.

Confusion

Sudden changes in mental awareness, confusion, or delirium — especially in adults over 65. A serious warning sign that requires immediate medical attention.

Prevention & Treatment

Protect & Recover

Proactive steps can significantly reduce risk. If infected, prompt treatment accelerates recovery.

Prevention

  • Vaccination — Pneumococcal, flu, and COVID-19 vaccines reduce risk significantly.
  • Hand Hygiene — Wash hands frequently with soap for at least 20 seconds.
  • No Smoking — Smoking damages lung defenses, increasing pneumonia risk.
  • Healthy Lifestyle — Regular exercise, balanced diet, and adequate sleep boost immunity.

Treatment

  • Antibiotics — For bacterial pneumonia; complete the full course as prescribed.
  • Antivirals — For viral pneumonia (e.g., influenza); antivirals reduce severity.
  • Rest & Hydration — Plenty of fluids and rest help your body recover.
  • Hospitalization — Severe cases may require oxygen therapy and IV antibiotics.

See a Doctor If...

  • Fever above 39°C (102°F) that won't come down with medication.
  • Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath at rest.
  • Chest pain, rapid heart rate, or bluish lips/fingertips.
  • Symptoms lasting more than 7 days or worsening after initial improvement.
  • Child under 2, adult over 65, or immunocompromised individual with any respiratory illness.
